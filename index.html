The question

Did the 2026 Hormuz war drain the world's oil reserves — or just shuffle them from buyers to sellers?

It drained them. For every 100 barrels oil-buying countries pulled from their reserves, only about 20 piled up unsold on the producer side. The other 80 were used up or never pumped at all — and refilling is slow because the bottleneck is the Strait, not the oilfields.

When Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz on 28 Feb 2026, Gulf producers couldn't store the oil they could no longer ship — their tanks filled within two weeks, so they shut wells rather than stockpile. Importers drew down strategic reserves to cope. This page walks the full picture: how depleted reserves are, who's exposed, where the missing oil went, and what refilling will take.

Is this actually unprecedented? — the US reserve in 50-year context

“Lowest since 1983” only means something if you can see the whole history. The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve was built up through the 1980s, peaked near 727 mb around 2009, was drawn hard in 2022, partly refilled — and this war has now pushed it back to where it stood in 1983, on the way up four decades ago.

Restocking outlook — the four questions

The core of the brief: how depleted, from where, at what price, and on what timeline.

Q1 · How depleted?

Severely, on the consumer side

SPR: 1983 low · OECD: 1990 low

Importers drew ~615 mb; demand destruction removed ~472 mb more. Producers couldn't hoard — they shut wells. The depletion is real and concentrated in strategic/commercial stocks, not a tidy redistribution.

Q2 · Restock from where?

Atlantic Basin + bypass pipes — not OPEC spare

The trap: OPEC's ~3.5–4 mb/d of spare capacity sits behind the Strait — it can be pumped but not shipped. It is not a usable restocking source while Hormuz is constrained.
Q3 · At what price?

≈ pre-war now, but forecasts split sharply

$74 today  vs $72 pre-war · $65 in late 2025

The war premium has bled out. But where it goes next depends entirely on whether the Strait stays open — see the forecaster split below.

Q4 · On what timeline?

Slow, back-loaded into 2027

Bypass routes are already maxed; no new capacity until 2027. With the usual ~4-month price-transmission lag, a meaningful refill is an H2-2026-into-2027 story — and only if the ceasefire holds. A re-closure resets the clock within weeks.

Why this matters

Restocking is second-order — at most ~166 mb either way, against a ~1,200 mb war-supply gap. It is not a big price-supportive overhang waiting to hit the market. That's why Brent can sit near $74 even with the US reserve at a 1983 low: the world used permanently less oil, so it doesn't need to buy most of it back.

Why refilling will be slow

Pipelines that route Gulf oil to the sea without the Strait can carry only a fraction of normal flow. Even with reserves low, the world can't quickly pump and ship replacements.
