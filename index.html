The question

Did the 2026 Hormuz war drain the world's oil reserves — or just shuffle them from buyers to sellers?

It drained them. For every 100 barrels that oil-buying countries pulled out of their reserves, only about 20 piled up unsold on the producer side. The other 80 were either used up or never pumped at all.

When Iran's strikes closed the Strait of Hormuz on 28 Feb 2026, Gulf producers couldn't store the oil they could no longer ship — their tanks filled within two weeks, so they shut wells instead of stockpiling. Importing countries drew down their strategic reserves to cope. Refilling is now the hard part, because the bottleneck is the Strait itself, not the oilfields.

Why refilling will be slow

Pipelines that route Gulf oil to the sea without using the Strait can only carry a fraction of what normally passes through it. So even with reserves low, the world can't quickly pump and ship replacement barrels.

The oilfields aren't the limit — the Strait is. Spare production capacity is stranded behind it.

Reserves are still falling — no turn yet
