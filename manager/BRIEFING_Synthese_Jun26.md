# Crude Reserves & Restocking — Synthèse Jun-2026

*Energy-markets briefing · as of 26 Jun 2026 · 118 days into the Iran–Hormuz conflict (war start 28 Feb).*

---

## Bottom line
The Hormuz closure **drained global reserves rather than relocating them.** Oil-buyers drew down their strategic/commercial stocks (US SPR at a 1983 low); producers could **not** stockpile the blocked oil (Gulf tanks filled in ~2 weeks → wells shut in), so the world's *total* reserves genuinely fell. Brent has round-tripped to ≈ pre-war (~$74), but **reserves have not started rebuilding** and won't materially until the Strait normalises — earliest Q4-2026.

**The counter-intuitive headline for your manager:** there is **no large "restocking race."** Demand destroyed (~472 mb) *exceeds* the entire refill need (~306 mb), so **net restocking demand is negative-to-negligible** — the world will buy back *less* than it drew. That is why prices are calm despite record-low strategic stocks. The real swing variable isn't who refills first; it's **whether the lost demand is permanent (bearish) or snaps back (bullish)** — and whether the fragile ceasefire holds the Strait open.

## ⚠️ Two things to flag before Monday
1. **Lead with *coverage*, not barrels.** The decision-relevant metric is **days / months of net-import cover**, not million barrels. A country can hold large volumes yet thin coverage, and vice-versa.
2. **Hormuz status is verified, not assumed** — and it's the swing factor. As of 26 Jun: **contested-but-flowing, ~4.8 mb/d vs ~15 prewar** (legally declared closed by Iran ~20 Jun, physically reduced-but-open under a fragile ceasefire). Every restocking conclusion is conditional on this.

## 2c · How each reserve actually drained (the mechanism)
The depletion is not a vague "consumers drew down" — it is the **IEA's 11-Mar 400 mb release executed country-by-country**, then *halted* as June crude was secured:
- **USA −80 mb** (411→331, 1983 low) = its 172 mb IEA pledge, partly executed. *But the US net-**exported** a record 5.8 mb/d in April* — the shock was coverage-**positive** for the US, which became the world's marginal replacement supplier.
- **Japan −80 mb** (15 days private + 1 month national, from March). Baseline was ~470 mb / ~254 days of consumption. **PM Takaichi declared no further releases after May** once June crude was secured → that is *why* the drawdown stopped at ~205 days.
- **South Korea** released a record **22.5 mb**; **Spain −11.5 mb** (−12.3 days, 17 Mar). Other IEA members ≈ 106 mb combined.
- **UK** holds **no government reserve** — its entire obligation is industry-held (Compulsory Stocking Obligation), met from ~76.6 Mbbl of obligated stock.

**Producer side — why it couldn't absorb the oil (two mechanisms):**
- **Saudi:** the binding constraint was **storage saturation**, not the transit ban — Ras Tanura/Ju'aymah/Abqaiq hit **90–95% full by early March** (Kayrros), forcing shut-in; **four supergiant offshore fields idled** (Safaniya, Marjan, Zuluf, Abu Safa ≈ 2–2.5 mb/d). Bypass is gated by **Yanbu *port* (~4 mb/d)**, not the 7 mb/d pipeline.
- **Qatar:** condensate is **co-produced with North Field gas** — Iranian strikes on Ras Laffan (force majeure; ~17% of LNG down for **3–5 years**) collapsed condensate *involuntarily*; Qatar's oil shut-in is partly a **gas-side casualty**, with zero bypass and only ~10 days of export cover.

## 3 · Restocking assessment
**a) How depleted, ~4 months in?** Severely on the **consumer/strategic side** — US SPR −19% (1983 low); OECD government stocks at their lowest since 1990. The **producer side did not absorb it**: Gulf tankage saturated within ~2 weeks, so **~700–850 mb** of output was **shut in at the wellhead** (time-weighted; the flat-rate ~1,080 mb assumes a full-118-day closure the flow data refutes — corrected after adversarial review), not stockpiled. Net global reserves fell genuinely.

**b) Restock from where?** **Atlantic Basin** crude (+~3.5 mb/d rerouted East-of-Suez: US, Brazil, Canada, Kazakhstan, Venezuela) plus **bypass pipelines** (Saudi Yanbu ~4 mb/d, UAE Fujairah ~1.5). **Not OPEC spare** — ~3.5–4 mb/d of it sits *behind* the Strait (Vitol: "all spare sits behind the Strait"). Note **Iraq's Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline gives near-zero relief** — it's north-only and doesn't connect to the Hormuz-exposed southern Basrah fields (>3.3 mb/d, which collapsed ~4.3→1.4 mb/d on storage saturation).

**c) At what price?** Brent ~$74 ≈ eve-of-war (~$72); the war premium has bled out. Forecasts are **bimodal, not a midpoint**: *deal holds* → ~$70–80 (Goldman ~$75, JPMorgan ~$64, Morgan Stanley ~$80, EIA ~$79); *deal fails / Strait re-closes* → ~$105–130 (RBC/Rapidan). The signal is the split, not the average.

**d) Timeline?** Slow and back-loaded. Bypass routes are already maxed (no new capacity until 2027); with the usual **~4-month price-transmission lag**, a meaningful refill is an **H2-2026-into-2027** event — and only if the ceasefire holds. A re-closure resets the clock within weeks.

## 4 · Total restocking-demand estimate — *is there even a "race"?*
| Component | mb | Basis |
|---|--:|---|
| Released stock to refill (SPR + IEA action, executed) | +186 | EIA SPR (80) + ~106 other-IEA |
| New strategic builds (genuinely stranded) | +120 | floating ~100 + onshore ~20 (Kpler/Vortexa) |
| **Gross restocking demand** | **+306** | |
| Less: demand permanently destroyed | −472 | IEA OMR (2Q26 −5 mb/d y/y; China+Japan imports −~40%) |
| **Net restocking demand** | **−166 to +48** | −166 on executed-draw basis; +48 if the full 400 mb authorisation is a standing obligation |

**The load-bearing finding — the "restocking race" is largely a myth for this episode.** Net restocking demand is **negative-to-negligible**: demand destruction (472 mb) *exceeds* the entire gross refill need (306 mb). The world permanently consumes **less** oil, so most of what was drawn doesn't need re-buying. That is why Brent sits at ~$74 **despite** the SPR at a 1983 low — there is no large price-supportive refill overhang waiting to hit the market. The competition for barrels is real at the margin (China's opportunistic buying vs. SPR-refill vs. commercial restock), but it is a **second-order** force against the ~1,200 mb gap, not a bidding war. *The binding uncertainty is whether the demand destruction is permanent (bearish) or snaps back (bullish) — not who refills first.*

## Data basis & honest caveat
- **Metric:** days/months of net-import cover (per the brief — coverage, not barrels). Net exporters (US, Netherlands) carry "–" because the import-cover metric is undefined for them; the **US SPR is tracked separately in §2**.
- **"Today" figures are estimates (`EST.`)** — expected and accepted under the brief. They are **agency/wire-attributed**, assembled from IEA/EIA/OPEC commentary and reputable press, **not pulled from the primary portals** (which were unreachable at build time). Treat exact figures as directional; **re-pull IEA OMR / EIA before any number is quoted externally.**
- **Confidence basis** is shown per cell (V verified / W wire / E modelled-estimate). Official and estimate are never blended in one cell.
- **Robust conclusions** (drained-not-redistributed; Europe down / Asia held; OPEC-spare trapped; refill is throughput-limited, H2-26/27): these hold regardless of decimal precision. The precise per-country days do not.
